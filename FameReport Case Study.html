01 — Problem & Context

Credit for MSMEs was
broken by invisible friction

India has over 63 million MSMEs, yet fewer than 14% have access to formal credit. The gap isn't always risk — it's information asymmetry. Lenders lacked a fast, trustworthy way to evaluate small businesses, and business owners had no clear picture of their own creditworthiness.

FinAGG Technologies — the parent company behind famescore.ai — built an invoice financing platform for MSME supply chains. As volumes grew, their lending partners demanded a standardised, auditable evaluation artefact before each disbursement. That artefact became FameReport.

💡 The core tension: Lenders needed depth and verifiability. MSMEs needed clarity and speed. Any report that served one group at the expense of the other would fail both.

🏦

Lender pain

Manual underwriting took 7–21 days. Loan officers reconciled GST filings, bank statements, and bureau reports from different portals by hand.

🏭

MSME pain

Business owners submitted the same documents to every lender separately. Rejections arrived with no explanation of which metric caused failure.

⚙️

Platform pain

FinAGG's internal credit team rebuilt evaluation logic for each lender partner — no single source of truth, expensive to maintain at scale.

Constraints we designed within

Regulatory

Data sourced from GSTIN, Experian (commercial + consumer), UDYAM, and Account Aggregator-linked bank statements. Each source has consent, retention, and display obligations under RBI and GSTN guidelines.

Technical

Report must render correctly as both a live web page and a downloadable PDF. Data is pulled fresh on each request — no stale cache. Partial data states (no bank statement linked, QRMP filer) must be handled gracefully.

Data fidelity

All figures are AI-calculated from raw filings. The report must be honest about data gaps (YTF, NC, NF), avoid false precision, and never show calculated values that contradict source documents.

Audience variance

The same report is consumed by a credit analyst at a large NBFC, a branch manager at a co-operative bank, and the MSME owner themselves — three very different mental models for the same data.

Key insights from research

"I open four tabs — GST portal, CIBIL, the borrower's bank statement, and our internal CRM — and I copy numbers into a spreadsheet. That's my 'underwriting'."

— Credit analyst, NBFC partner, user interview

🔍 Insight 1: Lenders trust sources, not summaries

Every number in the report must be traceable to its raw source (GSTR1 filing, Experian record, bank statement debit). Black-box AI scores without evidence were dismissed.

📅 Insight 2: Time-window matters enormously

Trailing-12-month (TTM) is the standard credit window, but lenders also want quarterly breakdowns to spot seasonal businesses hiding revenue drops.

🔗 Insight 3: Cross-data matching is the killer feature

Matching GST sales to bank credits (BTO%) was the single highest-value check. Lenders described it as the first thing they look for — and nobody else was doing it automatically.

📍 Insight 4: Geography and customer concentration are credit risk

A business with 66% sales in one state and 47% to one customer is fragile. Visualising this as Pareto charts was an "aha" moment in testing.

Jobs to be done

Verify, don't trust

"When I'm reviewing a loan file, help me catch discrepancies between what the borrower claims and what the data shows — before I commit credit."

Summarise fast

"When I have 12 files to review by EOD, give me the three numbers that matter most up front so I can prioritise deep dives."

Explain simply

"When I receive my FAME report as a business owner, help me understand what's holding me back and what I can fix before my next loan application."

04 — Ideation & Exploration

From whiteboard chaos
to a structured decision

With 28 data categories identified from research, the central design problem was: how do you organise a 39-section document so that a time-pressed analyst can find what they need in under 60 seconds?

Three structural concepts we explored

CONCEPT A — REJECTED

Narrative / storytelling

One continuous prose summary with data embedded. Felt "human" but failed lender usability tests — analysts couldn't scan to specific sections quickly. Ctrl+F dependency is a red flag.

CONCEPT B — CHOSEN ✓

Module-based with anchor nav

Discrete, named sections each with their own visual language. A summary "scorecard" at the top with jump links. Tested highest with analysts — they could skip directly to Sales Exposure or Bureau Info.

CONCEPT C — PARTIAL ADOPT

Risk-first dashboard

All red flags surfaced at page 1. Good for risk triage but removed context. We adopted this concept only for the Final Compliance Report section (page 38), not as the primary layout.

Decision matrix — section ordering

We mapped each data category on two axes: time-sensitivity (does staleness matter?) and decision weight (how much does this move the credit decision?). Sections in the top-right quadrant moved to the front of the report.

Micro-interactions & data-state patterns

Empty / partial data states

When a data source isn't linked (e.g. no bank statement), we display a — Not linked state with a contextual explanation, never a blank or error. Filing statuses use the abbreviations NC / NF / YTF / QF consistently throughout.

Cross-source match indicators

The Bureau-to-Bank loan matching table uses a 4-colour legend (Matched / Bureau only / Bank only / No match) that works in print using both colour and a pattern indicator — critical for WCAG compliance in PDF exports.

BTO% (Bank-to-Turnover) callouts

When BTO% is 100%, a ✓ Verified chip appears inline with the sales row. When below 80%, an ⚠ Review chip draws analyst attention without blocking the data.

Related-party flagging

Customers or suppliers where a PAN match against the entity's own group is detected are inline-flagged with a RPT badge. This surfaced in research as a top fraud-detection need.

Spacing & layout grid

The report uses an 8pt base grid. Section padding is 32px (4u), card padding is 24px (3u), and micro-gaps between inline elements are 8px (1u). For PDF, an A4 page at 96dpi maps this to physical millimetres, ensuring consistent print output. Column widths follow a 12-column system at 620px content width with 16px gutters.

What I'd do differently

  • Earlier MSME-owner testing. Most of our testing was with lenders. The MSME-owner section (plain-language summary) was under-tested and arrived as a v1.2 addition 8 weeks post-launch.
  • PDF-first constraint earlier. The PDF rendering layer introduced layout regressions that required two redesign sprints. PDF should have been a first-class design constraint from week one.
  • Modular report sections. A lender evaluating a micro-enterprise doesn't need the same 39 sections as one evaluating a ₹24 Cr turnover company. A tiered report length (Micro / Small / Medium) would improve relevance.
  • Accessibility deeper. The report meets WCAG AA for colour contrast but screen-reader support for the PDF is limited. This is the biggest open item for v2.
